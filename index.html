Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions and concerns addressed with facts

Can the federal government bypass local zoning laws?

The short answer: While federal facilities have some exemptions from local zoning ordinances, this doesn't mean communities have no say or leverage.

The full picture:

  • The City of Surprise controls critical infrastructure including water, sewer, and electrical connections
  • Converting a distribution warehouse into a detention facility triggers building code reviews and fire safety requirements
  • Federal law (NEPA - National Environmental Policy Act) requires environmental assessments for major federal actions, including community input
  • Recent examples show community opposition works: Oklahoma City and Hanover County, Virginia both successfully stopped similar 1,500-bed ICE facilities in January 2026
Isn't this decision already final?

No. While DHS purchased the building, converting it into an operational facility requires:

  • Building code compliance reviews
  • Utility connection agreements
  • Environmental assessments (NEPA)
  • Emergency services coordination

The Oklahoma City facility was cancelled even AFTER the property was under consideration, demonstrating that community input matters.

What actual leverage does Surprise have?

The city controls several critical elements:

  • Utilities: Water, sewer, and electrical infrastructure
  • Public safety: Police, fire, and emergency medical response coordination
  • Infrastructure: Roads, traffic signals, and access to the site
  • Building standards: Fire safety, health codes, and occupancy requirements

Operating a 1,500-person facility without local cooperation would be extremely difficult and expensive.

Won't this just divide the community?

This isn't about immigration policy - it's about transparency and community input. The City of Surprise was not consulted before a $70 million purchase that will impact:

  • A K-8 school with 1,868 students on the same street
  • Over 8,000 residents in nearby neighborhoods
  • Local traffic, emergency services, and infrastructure

Residents across the political spectrum agree: our community deserves a voice in decisions that affect our schools, neighborhoods, and quality of life.

What's the goal here?

We're asking the City of Surprise to:

  • Formally oppose this facility (as Hanover County did)
  • Demand a full impact study (traffic, emergency services, property values)
  • Request NEPA environmental review with community input
  • Negotiate conditions if the facility moves forward (impact fees, operational limits)
  • Engage state and federal representatives on our community's behalf

This is about ensuring our voices are heard in the process.

Has community opposition ever actually worked?

Yes. Recent examples include:

Oklahoma City (January 2026): Proposed 1,500-bed facility cancelled after community testified at city council meetings and property owners withdrew.

Hanover County, Virginia (January 2026): Board of Supervisors formally opposed facility, citing infrastructure concerns. Property owner withdrew from sale.

Multiple communities: Have successfully negotiated impact fees, operational restrictions, or alternative sites through sustained organizing.
