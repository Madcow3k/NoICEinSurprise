🚨 April 22, 2026: Federal Stop Work Order Halts All Construction at the Surprise Facility. Reason Not Disclosed. ▼

A federal stop work order halted all work on the Surprise facility on April 22, two days before Arizona Attorney General Kris Mayes filed suit in federal court. The order was issued to GardaWorld Federal Services and is documented in USAspending.gov, the federal procurement database. DHS, ICE, and GardaWorld have not stated a reason publicly.

  • 📜 Two contract modifications on record. Modification P00001, signed April 22, initiated the halt. Modification P00002, signed April 23, bore the explicit federal description: "THIS MODIFICATION UPDATES THE STOP WORK ORDER IN EFFECT AT THE SURPRISE PROCESSING AND DETENTION FACILITY." Both reflect a $0 obligation, consistent with administrative actions that carry no new funding.
  • 📅 The order preceded the AG's lawsuit by two days. AG Mayes filed suit on April 24. The stop work order was already in place. Mayes' office declined to comment on the stop work order, citing pending litigation.
  • 🗣️ Councilmember Judd confirmed the city manager verified the work stoppage with local ICE officials. Judd learned of it through a news article Monday, April 27. He then confirmed it through the city manager. "To actually see that they have put the contractor on hold is promising," he said.
  • ❓ Reason unknown. DHS, ICE, and GardaWorld have not responded to requests for comment. The duration of the order is also unknown.
  • 📍 Maryland precedent. A stop work order was issued to the contractor at the nearly identical Williamsport, Maryland facility on April 2, following that state's legal challenge. The Maryland federal court granted a preliminary injunction on April 15. In Maryland, the sequence was: lawsuit, then stop work order, then preliminary injunction. In Surprise, the stop work order arrived before the lawsuit was filed.
  • 🔍 First reported by Project Salt Box, an initiative that tracks federal detention procurement through USAspending.gov. AZPM and KJZZ confirmed independently through city officials and federal records.
“We were told through various sources that there was some sort of stoppage, for various reasons, you know, all speculative. So to actually see that they have put the contractor on hold is promising.”Councilmember Chris Judd, April 27, 2026

🚨 April 24, 2026: Arizona Attorney General Sues DHS and ICE in Federal Court Over the Surprise Facility ▼

Arizona Attorney General Kris Mayes filed suit today in federal court against the U.S. Department of Homeland Security (DHS), DHS Secretary Markwayne Mullin, U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), and ICE Acting Director Todd Lyons. The complaint alleges the defendants failed to conduct or publicize the environmental reviews required under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) before proceeding with the Surprise facility. It further alleges the facility violates the Immigration and Nationality Act (INA), which requires the federal government to arrange “appropriate” places for immigration detention. Mayes announced the suit at the site, near Sweetwater Avenue and Dysart Road, with State Senators Catherine Miranda and Analise Ortiz and Dysart High School Student Body Vice President Cali Overs in attendance.

  • ⚖️ Two federal claims. NEPA violation (no environmental impact statement, no environmental assessment, no categorical exclusion identified) and INA violation (the warehouse is not an “appropriate” place for detention).
  • ☢️ The chemical warehouse is central to the state’s case. The press release describes the site as “a potential chemical hazard zone” and states the Surprise warehouse sits “directly across the street from a facility storing thousands of gallons of hazardous chemicals.” The complaint cites the risk of holding a captive population adjacent to a Risk Management Plan facility.
  • 📅 New detail from the state’s filing: the chemical facility’s Risk Management Plan was filed on January 1, 2026. That is three weeks before the announcement of the ICE facility. According to the press release, the plan “does not consider the potential hazards made possible by the presence of a mass detention facility next door.”
  • 🏗️ Infrastructure concerns acknowledged at the state level. The filing notes the warehouse was built as an industrial distribution facility for up to four commercial tenants and “almost certainly lacks the water and wastewater infrastructure needed to safely house” hundreds to 1,500 people.
  • 📜 Public-nuisance theory dropped. Mayes confirmed on the record that she abandoned the public-nuisance approach floated in her February letter to then-Secretary Noem, citing the U.S. Constitution’s Supremacy Clause. The suit filed today is in federal court.
  • 📍 Direct precedent in Maryland. On April 15, 2026, U.S. District Judge Brenden Hurson granted Maryland’s motion for a preliminary injunction halting construction and operation of a nearly identical warehouse-to-detention-facility conversion in Washington County. That injunction extended the March 11 temporary restraining order in the same case. The Maryland suit relies on the same NEPA theory Mayes has now raised in Arizona.
  • 🛑 Filing does not stop construction on its own. In Maryland, separate motions were required to obtain first the TRO and then the preliminary injunction. Work at the Surprise site continues; steel security gates were installed during the week of April 21.
“The federal government did not ask the people of Surprise whether they wanted this facility in their backyards. They simply bought a warehouse, handed a $300 million contract to a private company and told the City to deal with it.”Arizona Attorney General Kris Mayes, April 24, 2026

🚨 April 9, 2026: Congressional Oversight Finds Overcrowding “Like Sardines” at the Nearest Existing Arizona ICE Facility ▼

On Thursday evening April 9, Reps. Greg Stanton, Yassamin Ansari, and Adelita Grijalva conducted an unannounced oversight visit at the Arizona Removal Operations Coordination Center (AROCC) at Phoenix-Mesa Gateway Airport. AROCC is an ICE holding facility on the transfer path for people who would cycle through the Surprise facility if it opens. It was designed for 157-person capacity and hold times of less than 12 hours. The oversight visit, prompted by an Arizona Mirror investigation published earlier the same day, found the facility operating far beyond those design parameters. DHS publicly denied the reported conditions, calling the reports “misinformation.”

  • 📊 Designed capacity: 157 people. Arizona Mirror’s analysis of ICE data shows an average daily population of 274 in 2026, with a single-day peak of 777.
  • ⏱️ Designed hold time: less than 12 hours. Average 2026 length of stay is 36 hours. At least one detainee was held 18 days.
  • 🛏️ No beds. No showers. Rep. Stanton described concrete rooms with no bedding or blankets, packed with people laying on the floor. A supervisor told the congressional delegation the facility holds people up to 72 hours despite the lack of sleeping or sanitation infrastructure.
  • 🔄 Detainees relocated before a prior inspection. Arizona Mirror’s analysis of ICE records found that when Stanton and Ansari gave the required seven days’ notice for a February oversight visit, the detainee population dropped to some of its lowest levels of 2026, then rose again almost immediately after the inspection. The same pattern has been documented at facilities in Maryland, Minnesota, and California.
  • 📥 Baltimore detainees transferred here. After a lawsuit forced a temporary shutdown of an ICE facility in Baltimore, detainees from that facility were transferred to AROCC, contributing to the overcrowding that prompted this oversight.
  • 📮 Formal letter to DHS Secretary Mullin. On April 10, the three representatives sent a formal letter demanding accountability and an explanation for holding people “well beyond the facility’s intended short-term holding period.”
  • 🗣️ DHS response: “Their claims this facility is overcrowded and horrific are FALSE. U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement operates under federal standards, oversight, and accountability, and claims of chaotic or inhumane conditions are misinformation.”
“What we saw tonight inside the facility was shocking. [The conditions were] significantly overcrowded.”Rep. Greg Stanton (AZ-04), April 9, 2026
“What we saw was utterly horrific. People are being packed like animals into grossly overcrowded spaces. The conditions are horrendous; no human being should be treated like this, not in America, not anywhere.”Rep. Adelita Grijalva (AZ-07), April 9, 2026

Why it matters to Surprise: AROCC is the current answer to what a short-term ICE holding facility in Arizona looks like in practice. It operates under the same federal standards DHS cites to reassure Surprise residents. DHS’s verbal commitments for the Surprise facility are the same kind of commitments being publicly contradicted 40 miles away, this week.

Yes. The city has more leverage than it has used so far.
